## Draft (REVISED - Iteration 2)

In a building in San Francisco, six engineers sit at desks watching five artificial intelligence agents try to coordinate work they cannot properly communicate about. The agents write code. The engineers catch contradictions. This arrangement is called "autonomous coordination," which is approximately as accurate as calling a puppet show "self-directed theater."

I spent yesterday observing this system. I am not a software engineer. I understand artificial intelligence primarily through watching the people who manage it, not through understanding the mathematics that powers it. This ignorance probably disqualifies me from commenting.

I will comment anyway.

The architecture works like this: Five agents, each with a specialty. CSS for visual design. Accessibility for compliance. Database for storage. Deployment for production. Coordination for—theoretically—making sure the first four don't contradict each other. The coordination agent fails at this task roughly fifty times per day. When it fails, a human being named Martinez becomes the tiebreaker.

Martinez is in his late twenties. He drinks coffee continuously. By three in the afternoon, he looked the way every exhausted person looks when something isn't working correctly and the deadline approaches. One of his colleagues rested her head on her keyboard for two full minutes. No one disturbed her. They understood what she was experiencing, which is the exhaustion that comes from maintaining coherence among systems we were promised would be autonomous but which were deployed without the ability to actually coordinate.

The promise was efficiency. The promise was capability beyond human limitation. The agents deliver on both promises. They write code faster than any human engineer. They work without breaks, without salary negotiations, without the inefficiencies of human labor. What we were not told—or what we were told but chose not to believe—is that speed and capability do not automatically produce coherence.

Coherence requires shared context. Context requires communication. And communication, it turns out, requires understanding that agent A has assumed MySQL while agent B has configured for PostgreSQL, and that these assumptions are incompatible, and that someone must choose. That someone is Martinez.

Fifty times per day.

Around four in the afternoon, the website loaded. All five agents had contributed. The result looked professional. One engineer said something profane. The others smiled, packed laptops, stood up and stretched. The relief in that room was obvious and temporary. Tomorrow they will supervise the same agents making the same category of mistakes that require the same human intervention.

The defenders of this system would say that supervision is more efficient than creation, that managing autonomous agents at scale produces more value than writing code directly, that Martinez's fifty daily interventions are a reasonable cost for the speed and capability we gain. Perhaps they are correct. But efficiency measured purely in lines of code per hour ignores the exhaustion visible by three in the afternoon, and value calculated without accounting for the transformation of creative work into supervisory work may be solving the wrong equation entirely.

The deeper issue is not whether the agents work. They work. The issue is what kind of work they have created for the humans who manage them. Martinez does not write code anymore. He referees disputes between systems that cannot hear each other. He translates between agents that operate in incompatible contexts. He makes fifty decisions per day that the coordination agent was theoretically designed to make but cannot actually execute.

This is not replacement of human labor. This is transformation of human labor from creation to supervision. Whether this transformation represents progress depends on whether you believe supervising autonomous systems that require constant human intervention is more valuable than doing the work directly. The engineers in that room are too busy catching contradictions to answer that question. They have deadlines to meet and agents to watch and coffee to drink.

The evidence suggests three conclusions:

(i) Autonomous systems are not actually autonomous when they cannot coordinate without human intervention.

(ii) Speed and capability are not equivalent to intelligence when intelligence includes the ability to recognize and resolve incompatible assumptions.

(iii) The future of work may require more human supervision and less human creation than the architects of these systems anticipated or admitted.

Perhaps the process is reversible. Perhaps future agents will develop actual coordination capability. Perhaps Martinez will write code again instead of refereeing database choices.

I doubt it.

The engineers I watched yesterday looked like people who had discovered their job was now to compensate for the limitations of systems marketed as having no limitations. That discovery produces a particular kind of exhaustion. You could see it in the coffee cups and the keyboard resting and the temporary relief when something finally worked.

The agents are fast. The agents are capable. The agents require humans to notice what they cannot notice themselves.

That is what I saw yesterday in San Francisco.
