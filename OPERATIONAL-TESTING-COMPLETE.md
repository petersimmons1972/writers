## Lessons Learned

### What Worked Exceptionally Well

1. **Zero-defect pattern extraction**
   - Rickover's systematic measurement created trust
   - Every guideline traceable to evidence
   - No subjective "write authentically" advice

2. **3-tier guideline structure**
   - Clear what's mandatory vs. optional
   - Enables consistent voice without rigidity
   - Appropriate flexibility for context

3. **Comparative framework**
   - Showing Murrow vs. Pyle distinctions helps voice selection
   - Prevents accidental voice mixing
   - Highlights key differentiators

4. **Testing on same topic**
   - Identical subject matter highlights voice differences
   - Validates voices are distinct, not just different topics
   - Shows adaptability to modern domains

### What Could Be Enhanced

1. **Compound "and" chains** (Pyle)
   - Test had 1-2, target is higher (12-15 per 1000 words)
   - Could be more emphasized in guidelines

2. **"There are no words" technique** (Murrow)
   - Powerful but wasn't needed for test topic
   - Could clarify when this flourish is appropriate

3. **Automated scoring**
   - Manual validation works but is time-consuming
   - Could build tools to automate rubric scoring

### Unexpected Discoveries

1. **One-sentence paragraphs are THE Pyle marker**
   - Most immediately recognizable structural difference
   - Zero in Murrow, 20-30% in Pyle
   - Single metric distinguishes voices

2. **Voice applies to any domain**
   - WWII correspondent voices work for AI topics
   - Voice is not domain-specific
   - Perspective/structure matter more than subject knowledge

3. **Anti-patterns prevent voice drift**
   - "Never do X" is as powerful as "Always do Y"
   - Negative constraints maintain boundaries
   - Guidelines need both positive and negative rules
