### 1.3 Rhythm Patterns

**Pattern 1: Short-Long-Short (found in 7/8 samples = 87.5%)**
- Sample 1: "That's all he said" (4 words) → long compound → short follow-up
- Sample 3: "Sure." (1) → "Hell yes." (2) → preceding long description

**Pattern 2: Build-Up Compound Chains (found in 8/8 samples = 100%)**

Pyle's signature: compound clauses joined by "and" building to emotional peak.

Example from Sample 1:
"And finally he put the hand down, and then reached up and gently straightened the points of the captain's shirt collar, and then he sort of rearranged the tattered edges of his uniform around the wound. And then he got up and walked away down the road in the moonlight, all alone."

**Compound chain frequency per sample:**
- Sample 1: 23 chains | Sample 2: 18 | Sample 3: 15 | Sample 4: 12
- Sample 5: 27 (highest - London Blitz, most lyrical)
- Sample 6: 9 | Sample 7: 3 | Sample 8: 4

**Average:** 13.9 compound "and" chains per 1000 words

**Pattern 3: Fragment-After-Buildup (found in 6/8 samples = 75%)**
- Sample 3: Long description → "The good-luck emblem. Sure. Hell yes."
- Sample 2: Long description → "They are too far away now."

**Key Finding:** Pyle's rhythm is DELIBERATE ASYMMETRY. Never mechanical alternation. Pattern: accumulate detail → release tension → reset.
