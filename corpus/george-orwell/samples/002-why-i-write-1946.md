# Why I Write (1946)

**Source:** https://www.orwell.ru/library/essays/wiw/english/e_wiw
**Date:** 1946
**Genre:** Essay (Personal/Reflective)
**Word Count:** ~3,500 words

## Summary

Orwell's 1946 essay explains his motivations for writing, beginning with his childhood recognition that he would become a writer. He describes how isolation and unpopularity shaped his literary ambitions from an early age.

## Four Primary Motives for Writing

Orwell identifies four key impulses that drive prose writers:

1. **Egoism** — the desire for recognition, remembrance, and validation
2. **Aesthetic enthusiasm** — appreciation for beauty in language and form
3. **Historical impulse** — documenting truth and facts for posterity
4. **Political purpose** — influencing society toward desired change

## Key Argument

Orwell contends that "every line of serious work that I have written since 1936 has been written, directly or indirectly, against totalitarianism and for democratic socialism." He emphasizes that writers cannot remain politically neutral and that consciousness of one's bias actually strengthens both political and artistic integrity.

## Central Tension

He acknowledges the challenge of balancing aesthetic concerns with urgent political messaging, noting that "good prose is like a windowpane"—transparent in its communication rather than ornate.

The essay ultimately suggests that honest political purpose, combined with disciplined craft, produces the writer's most meaningful work.

## Voice Characteristics
- Brutally honest self-examination
- Willing to admit unflattering motivations (egoism)
- Numbered lists for clarity
- Metaphor: "good prose is like a windowpane"
- Connects personal history to broader principles
- Acknowledges contradictions without resolving them
