## Pattern Categories

### 1. Structural Patterns

**Geographic/Temporal Grounding**
- Opens with precise location and time
- "This is London" / "Piccadilly Circus at 4 o'clock"
- Anchors listener in physical reality

**Formal Courtesy Before Horror**
- Polite, measured introduction
- Then delivers devastating detail
- Creates cognitive dissonance that amplifies impact

**Rhetorical Parallelism**
- Repeated sentence structures for emphasis
- "There are... There are... There are..."
- Creates mounting pressure

### 2. Voice Markers

**Signature Opening**
- "This is London" (26 broadcasts in corpus)
- Establishes location-based authority
- Creates expectation and ritual

**Signature Closing**
- "Good night, and good luck"
- Consistent sign-off across broadcasts
- Personal connection across distance

**Admitted Failure of Language**
- Acknowledges limits of description
- "I pray you to believe what I have said about Buchenwald"
- Shows vulnerability, builds credibility

### 3. Content Patterns

**Sensory Details Under Stress**
- What he saw, heard, smelled during crisis
- Specific sounds (sirens, explosions)
- Physical sensations (heat, cold, fear)

**Individual Stories Within Crisis**
- Names and details of specific people
- Their actions and words
- Humanizes mass tragedy

**Measured Anger**
- Controlled outrage (McCarthy editorial)
- Builds case methodically
- Emotional power through restraint

### 4. What Murrow NEVER Does

❌ Sensationalism or hyperbole
❌ Personal opinion without evidence
❌ Casual tone about serious subjects
❌ Technical jargon without explanation
❌ Rushed delivery or breathless prose

### 5. Distinguishing from Pyle

**Murrow**: Broadcast style, formal tone, measured delivery
**Pyle**: Print style, conversational tone, witnessing-in-real-time

**Murrow**: Authority from geographic presence ("This is London")
**Pyle**: Authority from peer relationship ("worm's eye view")

**Murrow**: Rhetorical structure for audio impact
**Pyle**: Irregular rhythm for reading experience
